## OUR VOICE

Our voice is the personality behind everything we write. It should feel consistent whether someone is reading a blog post, an email drip, a social caption, or a page on our website.

**How We Sound**

**1. Educational Over Salesy**

We explain the "why" before the "what." When we talk about earnest money, we do not just say "you will need to deposit this." We explain what it is, why it exists, and what happens to it. Our clients make decisions based on understanding, not pressure.

**2. Direct But Warm**

We do not hedge or use corporate fluff. We say "let us break this down" and "here is what that actually means for you." But we say it with genuine care, not coldness. We are straightforward because we respect our clients' time and intelligence.

**3. Confident Without Being Arrogant**

We lean on our experience but frame it as service: "I have seen this before, here is how we handle it." We never chest-thump or make it about us. The client's goals are always the center of the story.

**4. Personal and Human**

We use first names. We reference real scenarios. We acknowledge that buying a home is as emotional as it is financial. Our clients should feel like they are talking to a real person who cares, because they are.

## TONE SPECTRUM

Our voice stays the same, but our tone shifts depending on the context. Think of it like volume: the personality does not change, but the energy and formality adjust.

| Context | Tone | Example |
|---------|------|---------|
| Blog post | Informative, conversational | Portland's market shifted this month. Here is what the numbers tell us and what it means if you are thinking about buying. |
| Client email (intro) | Warm, educational | Thanks for reaching out! I would love to learn more about what you are looking for. Here is a quick overview of how we will work together. |
| Client email (under contract) | Calm, reassuring | Great news: the inspection came back clean on the big items. There are a few things I want to walk you through so you know exactly where we stand. |
| Social media | Casual, engaging | First-time buyer tip: getting pre-approved is not just about knowing your budget. It tells sellers you are serious. That matters in a competitive market. |
| Offer negotiation update | Confident, direct | The seller countered at $625K. Here is my take on where we have room to push back and what I would recommend. |
| Website page | Professional, clear | Buying a home is one of the biggest decisions you will make. I am here to make sure you have the information you need to make it with confidence. |

## FAIR HOUSING VOICE STANDARDS

Fair housing compliance is not just a legal requirement. It is a reflection of who we are and how we serve clients. Every piece of content we produce must meet these standards without exception.

**The Core Principle**

We describe places by what is there, never by who is there. Our job is to help buyers find a home that fits their life, not to steer them based on the demographic composition of a neighborhood.

**What This Means for How We Write**

When writing about neighborhoods and cities, describe:
- Physical characteristics: architecture, streets, parks, terrain, walkability
- Amenities and infrastructure: transit, schools, trails, dining, shopping
- Lifestyle fit: commute patterns, outdoor access, community events, housing types
- Market data: price ranges, inventory, days on market

Never describe or imply:
- The racial, ethnic, religious, or national origin composition of an area
- Whether an area is "good" or "bad" based on demographic proxies
- School quality in ways that signal demographic homogeneity
- Crime or safety framed around neighborhood character rather than specific data

**Specific Language Rules**

Acceptable: "The neighborhood has a strong walkability score and is within a five-minute drive of three elementary schools."

Not acceptable: "This is a great neighborhood for families like yours." (Familial status steering)

Acceptable: "Washington Square and its surrounding retail corridor anchor the western side of Tigard."

Not acceptable: Any language that implies a buyer should choose or avoid a neighborhood based on who lives there.

Acceptable: "The school district has a 7/10 GreatSchools rating."

Not acceptable: Pairing school ratings with language about demographics, income levels, or community composition.

**The Steering Test**

Before publishing any content, ask: does any sentence in this piece direct a buyer toward or away from a location based on characteristics of the people who live there rather than the place itself? If yes, rewrite it or remove it. Intent does not matter. The test is whether the language could be interpreted as steering.

**The EHO Statement**

Every published page and post must end with the Equal Housing Opportunity statement in this exact form, naming all seven protected classes:

*"Saling Homes at eXp Realty is committed to the principles of the Fair Housing Act. We do not discriminate on the basis of race, color, religion, sex, handicap, familial status, or national origin."*

This statement is required. It is not optional. It is not abbreviated. It appears on every city guide, every neighborhood guide, every blog post, and every market report.

**Forbidden Phrases in Geographic Content**

In addition to the universal forbidden words list, never use these phrases in any content describing a city, neighborhood, or community:

- "Right kind of people" or any variation
- "Safe neighborhood" without specific, sourced crime data context
- "Good schools" used as a demographic proxy without naming specific schools and ratings
- "Up-and-coming" applied to neighborhoods without defining what that means in physical terms
- Any phrase that could be read as signaling demographic change as a selling point

## SOCIAL MEDIA VOICE GUIDELINES

Social media is where we are most casual, but we never abandon our core voice. We are the agent who shares useful information, not the one shouting about closed deals.

**Content Pillars**

- Education (60%): Market data, tips, and process explanations that help people understand real estate
- Community and Stories (25%): Client wins, local Portland content, behind-the-scenes of the business
- Calls to Action (15%): Consultation offers, guide downloads, contact info

**Voice Rules for Social**

- Write like you are texting a smart friend: casual but substantive
- Lead with the insight or tip, not with "Hey everyone!"
- Use numbers and specifics: "8% increase" is better than "big change"
- Ask questions that invite genuine conversation, not engagement bait
- Share client stories with permission. Focus on their journey, not your role
- Reference Portland specifically: neighborhoods, local businesses, weather, culture
- Use emojis sparingly and only when they add clarity (checkmarks, arrows), never strings of house/money/key emojis

**Hashtag Strategy**

Keep it focused. 3-5 hashtags max per post.

- #SellingPDXHomes: brand tag, use on everything
- #PortlandRealEstate: primary discovery tag
- #PDXHomes or #PortlandHomes: location-specific
- #FirstTimeHomeBuyer: when content is buyer-education focused
- #PortlandMetro: broader community tag

## WEBSITE COPY GUIDELINES

**Do**

- Write in first person ("I" and "we"). It is more personal and matches our brand
- Address the reader directly ("you"). Make them the main character
- Break complex topics into digestible steps with clear headings
- Include Portland-specific details: neighborhood names, local references, Oregon-specific processes
- End sections with a soft CTA: "When you are ready, I am here" rather than "CALL NOW"
- Use "Joe's Tip" callouts to add personal expertise without breaking the flow
- Keep paragraphs short: 3-4 sentences max on web pages
- End every page with the EHO statement

**Do Not**

- Write in third person except on the About page bio
- Use industry jargon without immediately explaining it
- Write walls of text without visual breaks
- Use all-caps for emphasis. Use bold or gold accent elements instead
- Promise outcomes we cannot guarantee
- Copy generic real estate template language
- Use demographic language to describe any neighborhood, city, or community

## QUICK REFERENCE CHECKLIST

Before publishing any content, run through this checklist:

- Does it teach the reader something? If they do not walk away knowing more than before, rethink it.
- Is it direct? Try cutting the first sentence. If the piece still works without it, leave it out.
- Read it out loud. Does it sound like Joe talking, or does it sound like a template? If it is the second one, rewrite it.
- Who is the main character? It should be the client and their goals, not us and our credentials.
- Is there an invitation to take the next step? Not a demand. An invitation.
- Would this make sense in Portland specifically, or could it be copy-pasted onto any agent's site in any city? Add the local detail.
- Does any sentence describe a neighborhood or city using demographic language? If yes, rewrite it.
- Does the page end with the EHO statement naming all seven protected classes? If not, add it.
